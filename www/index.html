About Holst

Gustav Theodore Holst was an English composer and teacher most famously known for composing 'The Planets'. Holst was born to Adolph von Holst, a professional musician, and his wife, Clara Cox on 21 September 1874 at 4 Pittville Terrace and passed away 25 May 1934. Other works by Holst include Beni Mora Suite, Reviving Purcell and Written in the stars.

The Music Room

This room is where Holst's piano is held. The piano was installed at his home in Thaxted, Essex. He bought it in 1913, second-hand, for £12. This piano is one of the pianos which would have been used to compose 'The Planets'.

The Holst Discovery Space

Whilst this room was opened in 2013, it room enables visitors to find out more about the composer through digitized archive material presented on a touch screen and a page-turning kiosk.

The Victorian Bedroom

It is possible that this is the room Gustav Holst was born in.. It is decorated in the style of the 1870s, with sombre furnishings.

Late Regency Sitting Room

This room, being the largest first floor room, was probably the dining room, but is presented today as a Regency period sitting room.

The Maid's Bedroom

To this day, we still know the names of the Holst maids - Clara Burrup and her successor Julia Giles.

The Nursery

It is highly likely that Holst's parents had a nursery which may have had two nursemaids in charge of children's care.

The Kitchen

This is an original Victorian kitchen containing a working kitchen range from a local Cheltenham property.

The Larder

In order to keep food fresh, food was stored in the larder. This is as fridges and freezers did not yet exist.

The Servant's Sitting Room

This was the room that the maid was allowed to rest in by the fire after having completed her long day of labour.

The Butler's Pantry

This was a room used to store household items.

The Scullery

1. 16 Rotunda Terrace

Gustavus and his family are listed in the 1861 census as living here. The family consisted of Gustavus, Honoria, Adolphus, Lorenz, Matthias, Nina and Katherine. It is likely that the house was also a shop where Gustavus dealt in harps. Gustavus brought up all his children to be musicians. His daughter Kate also taught at the Ladies’ College. His son Matthias was a renowned concert pianist and also apparently a heartthrob who went to a fancy dress party naked. Gustavus’ other son, Adolphus (Adolph) was also a talented musician. Adolph was well known in Cheltenham for being a concert pianist and also for organising concerts in a range of venues across the town. He was also the organist at All Saints’ Church in Cheltenham.

2. The Rotunda

The Rotunda was built as a pump room for taking the famous Cheltenham spa waters. It was also used as a concert venue with both Adolph and Gustav performing there. In 1891 the first performance was given of two juvenile works by the 16 year old Gustav Holst. They were listed in the programme as Scherzo and Intermezzo. The pieces were never published and the original autograph manuscripts are now in the Holst Birthplace Museum archives.

3. 5 Queen’s Circus

Before Gustavus von Holst lived with his family at 16 Rotunda Terrace, he lived at 5 Queen’s Circus. He wrote a letter in 1853 from 5 Queen’s Circus accepting the position as Professor of Music at Cheltenham Ladies College. This letter is now in the archives at the Cheltenham Ladies College archives. He describes in the letter how he is able to supply ‘first rate’ instruments (pianofortes etc) for purchase ‘which can be seen at my residence here where I have several.’

4. Statue of Gustav Holst by Anthony Stones

In 2008 Cheltenham Civic Society raised money to erect a statue to Gustav Holst, Cheltenham’s most famous son. The sculptor was Anthony Stones and it was cast at Pangolin Editions in Chalford, Stroud.

5. Cheltenham Town Hall

When Cheltenham’s main concert venue, the Assembly Rooms were demolished, Cheltenham Town Hall was built to provide a new venue for balls and concerts. Built in 1902-03, the Town Hall played an important part in the career and musical legacy of Gustav Holst. In 1927 it hosted the Holst Festival where Holst conducted The Planets. Holst later said the occasion was the most overwhelming event of his life. As well as the Holst Festival the Town Hall also hosted the British premiere of what Holst considered his best work, Egdon Heath in 1928. Today Cheltenham Town Hall remains a venue where Holst’s music is often performed, for example in recent years there have been performances there of The Planets, Savitri and Beni Mora.

6. 1 Vittoria Walk

1 Vittoria Walk, the house where the 7 year old Gustav Holst moved to with his father and brother after his mother died no longer stands. It is now the site of a BT Building. However in the 1891 census, Adolph and his family are listed as living here with Adolph’s second wife Mary Thorley Stone. Adolph is listed as teacher of piano and an organist. Gustav is listed as 16 and a ‘scholar’. His brother Emil, 14 is also listed as a ‘scholar’. At this point Gustav had two half-brothers, Matthias and Thorley, 4 and 1. At that time there were two live in servants: Sarah A Snow, aged 27, a cook born in Cheltenham, and Matilda Dickenson, aged 16, the children’s nurse, born in Notgrove, Gloucestershire.

7. 6 Montpellier Villas

In 1871 Adolph von Holst married Clara Lediard. When Clara met Adolph she lived with her widowed mother and sisters at 4 Pittville Terrace, now the Holst Birthplace Museum. The Lediard family were originally from Cirencester. Clara had been Adolph’s piano pupil. According to the Cheltenham Annuaire, a town directory, the newly married couple moved to this house. They lived here until 1874, just a few months before Gustav was born.

8. 45 Bath Road, ‘Oxford House’

In the 1901 census Adolph is listed as living in lodgings in this house. By this point in his life he was separated from his second wife who had emigrated to America. Gustav was now in London, having written several notable compositions including The Cotswold Symphony in 1900. Adolph died in this house following a visit to what is now The Montpellier Wine Bar.

9. 16 Cambray

Gustavus and Adolph lived here together in 1870. This is where Gustavus is listed as living when he died in 1870. At this point his wife Honoria had died and his children Matthias and Katherine were living in Glasgow as music teachers.

10. Lloyds Bank, High Street, site of the Assembly Rooms

The present building is on the site of Cheltenham’s Assembly Rooms, one of the town’s former concert halls. Adolph von Holst, Gustav’s father, organised concerts and performed here. Gustav himself also conducted sections from his Lansdown Castle here on 22nd December 1892.’

11. Marks and Spencer, site of Cheltenham Corn Exchange

Holst conducted his work Lansdown Castle in full at the Corn Exchange. This work was part of an unsuccessful scholarship application to the Royal College of Music. In 1893, after passing the entrance exams, he borrowed £100 from his father and left Cheltenham for London.

12. Cheltenham Chapel

inside Cheltenham Chapel due to a new school building being built. Holst was apparently teased at school for not being very good at sport, earning the nickname, ‘Sausage.’

13. All Saints Church

Adolph von Holst (1846-1901), Gustav Holst’s father was the first organist of All Saints Church. He and his wife Clara Lediard were married here in 1871, and both their sons, Gustav and Emil, were baptised here. Gustav sang in the choir and would also have played the organ, possibly to help him compose his early organ works.

Gustav Holst, composer of The Planets, was born in this Regency house in 1874. The Museum was set up in 1975 to celebrate Cheltenham’s most famous son and also to offer a glimpse into what life was like for an ordinary 19th century family living in the town. Step back in time as you wander through its historic rooms, including Holst’s Victorian bedroom, nursery, kitchen and scullery. Imagine Cheltenham’s Regency past in the only Regency room open to the public in the town, complete with hand blocked wallpaper, fine furnishings and 19th century art collection. See and listen to the piano Holst used to compose The Planets in a recreation of his music room. Delve deeper into his life in the Holst Discovery Space, through archives and film. Drop into the Learning Space for one of our regular family activities. We are a small, independent museum governed by the Holst Birthplace Trust, registered charity number: 1078599.
